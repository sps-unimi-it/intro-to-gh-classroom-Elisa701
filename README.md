# Elisa’s Exam — Replication Study: Sentiment Dictionaries on BBC News

Repository for the final project of the c4ss (Computational Social Science) course.
This repository holds the completed project of the Computational Social Science class. In this project, a replication of the proposed methodology by Chan et al. (2021) has been done by using three sentiment dictionaries, which are AFINN, Bing Liu, and NRC on a balanced set of 90 articles from BBC news. The main idea behind this study is to compare the sentiments through different lexicons and effect of normalization of scores on document-level sentiments.

## Methodology
The notebook follows the analytical workflow proposed by Chan et al. (2021):

- collect a balanced news corpus;
- preprocess textual data;
- compute sentiment using three off-the-shelf dictionaries;
- calculate both raw and normalized sentiment scores;
- compare dictionary agreement;
- evaluate the influence of document length on sentiment estimation.


## Lexicons Used

The three off‑the‑shelf dictionaries required for the exam are:

1. **AFINN-111** —  integer scores from −5 to +5 per word.
2. **Bing Liu** — lists of positive and negative words.
3. **NRC Emotion Lexicon** — associations of words with 8 emotions, including positive and negative.

The notebook looks for lexicons in `../downloaded_lexica/` (as in the original course structure).
If that folder is not available, it automatically falls back to the local copies in `Esame_Elisa/lexica/`.

## Notes

- Articles were downloaded from official BBC URLs and saved as  `.txt` files in `Esame_Elisa/Data/BBC_news/`.
- The analysis uses **length‑normalized scores** (`score / word_count`)to replicate the content‑length bias correction discussed in the reference study.
- Sport is the most positive category on average, health is the most negative while technology is neutral or slightly positive depending on the referred lexicon.
- Generally, the goal of the project is to emulate the methodology recommended by Chan et al. (2021), rather than duplicate the original data set, in order to show how various sentiment lexicons function on professionally written news articles.
